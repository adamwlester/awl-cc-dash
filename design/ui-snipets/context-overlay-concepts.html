Context · Rewind · Handoff

concepts

Four ways to surface the /context breakdown (your screenshot 2) while keeping the slim usage bar as the always-on readout, with Rewind and Handoff (screenshot 1) folded in as siblings. Every control below is live, click it. All use the v6.3 tokens. The core idea across all of them: Context, Rewind, and Handoff are three reads on session state, so they want one shared surface rather than three unrelated affordances.

The short version
Your hunch is right: tabs beat the loose button group. My ranking, with the tradeoff each one is buying:
  • DefaultIdea 1 — inline tri-tabs. Native to your rw-tabs, nothing floats, the bar stays put. Best if the breakdown can live in the rail.
  • If bigIdea 2 — modal. Faithful to screenshot 2, full room, rail never reflows. Best when memory files + agents lists grow long.
  • LightestIdea 3 — anchored popover. Closest to your split-menu pattern. No dim, no reflow, fast in-and-out.
  • BonusIdea 4 — shared bottom sheet. One resizable surface, three feeds. Best if you want history persistently parked, not transient.
1 Inline tri-tabs

One tab strip, three views, expands in place

The cleanest rework of your "strange buttons." A single Context · Rewind · Handoff strip (your exact rw-tabs styling). The slim usage bar sits above the tabs and never leaves, so the main-window readout always shows. Clicking a tab opens an accordion panel underneath; clicking the active tab again collapses it. No overlays, no floating layers.

Buys you

  • Zero new UI vocabulary, it's your timeline tabs plus one more tab
  • Bar is always visible as the collapsed summary
  • Keyboard / focus trivial, nothing to trap or dismiss

Costs you

  • An open panel pushes the rail content below it down
  • Long memory/agent lists need an inner scroll cap
  • Only one of the three open at a time

2 Modal overlay

Press Context, a focused panel opens over the workspace

What you literally described. The bar stays a slim row in the rail; the whole row is now a button. Pressing it opens a dimmed, centered panel that mirrors screenshot 2 exactly, model, token total, stacked bar, the full category table, memory files and custom agents. The Rewind / Handoff tabs open the same kind of overlay, pre-set to that verb, so the long point list never crowds the rail.

Buys you

  • The detail gets full room; lists can be long
  • Rail layout never shifts, the bar stays one line
  • Reads as a real "drill-in," matches /context mental model

Costs you

  • Heaviest interaction, dims and covers the workspace
  • Needs Esc / click-out / focus handling done properly
  • Overkill if you just want a glance

3 Anchored popover

A floating card, hung off the bar like your split-menu

The middle path, and the closest match to the dropdown chip pattern you already built. Clicking Context pops a card upward off the bar (same direction and shadow as split-menu) with the breakdown inside, no screen dim, no reflow. Rewind and Handoff each pop their own card the same way. Click elsewhere or hit Esc to dismiss.

Buys you

  • Lightest of the "doesn't move the layout" options
  • Reuses muscle memory from your split-button menu
  • Fast glance-and-go; one click to open, one to close

Costs you

  • A 7-row table plus two lists can feel cramped floating
  • Can clip near the viewport edge, needs flip logic
  • Less "official" than a modal for a primary readout

4 Shared bottom sheet

One drawer slides up; tabs swap what's in it

If you'd rather park history than summon it. A single sheet slides up over the rail with a grab handle; the Context · Rewind · Handoff strip lives in its header and swaps the contents of the one surface. The slim bar stays visible up top; selecting a tab raises the sheet, the handle drops it. One persistent, resizable region instead of three transient ones.

Buys you

  • One surface to design, style and scroll, three feeds
  • Feels deliberate and "dockable," good for long sessions
  • Handle invites resize; height is yours to set

Costs you

  • Covers the lower rail while open (partial dim)
  • Most moving parts of the four to build
  • Closest in spirit to Idea 1, may be redundant
